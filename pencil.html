Pencil fragment

Location: trench 1, context 3

This is a small, extremely plain wood cylinder, devoid of any markings or decoration. There is an off-center rod of graphite through the middle, and it is clear that the cylinder has broken and used to be longer on both sides. It appears to be a fragment of a pencil.

The pencil is perfectly round, lacking the classic hexagonal shape of most common modern pencils. It does not appear to be made of cedar, but rather some other type of wood that could be fir, poplar, or another species. Cedar is considered the best wood for pencil making, and is used in higher quality pencils. Other types of wood are generally cheaper, but lead to more splintering, difficulty sharpening, and worse pencil performance.[8] Additionally, the graphite that runs through the pencil is off center, indicating that this pencil is not high quality, and was likely mass produced at low cost.

Whether a resident of 82 Brown St bought the pencil to do schoolwork in the 19th century or if it was discarded by a frustrated art student in 2023 is impossible to determine, but we can conclude that the pencil was most likely purchased and used by a budget-conscious consumer who had a need to write things down.
