Chicken wire glass

Location: trench 1, context 3

These pieces of chicken wire glass were found in trench 1, but other shards of chicken wire glass were found in various contexts and other trenches throughout the LAB site. They are ribbed pieces of clear glass containing chicken wire, and the glass has become discolored over time as the embedded chicken wire has rusted.

Chicken wire glass was first invented in 1894, when the construction of the Tower Bridge London popularized glass-enclosed walkways, and safety became a major concern.[3] Manufacturers decided to embed metal chicken wire in glass, increasing the durability of the glass. Chicken wire glass was less prone to shattering then regular glass, and more importantly, was extremely resistant to fires and extreme temperatures.[3] The glass quickly rose in popularity, and became commonly used in schools, banks, hospitals, museums, airports, and other public buildings throughout the 20th century. However, although the chicken wire increases safety by holding the glass shards in place when the glass breaks, it actually decreases the strength of the glass, and was found to be extremely hazardous in high impact situations.[5]

As a result, the installation of chicken wire glass was banned in the US by a 2006 update to the US International Building Code.[5] The List Art Building was constructed in 1971, prior to this ban, so it is possible that chicken wire glass was used in either the construction of the List Art Building, or in the house at 82 Brown Street. In either case, the use of chicken wire indicates that those constructing the building were concerned about the safety of the occupants, especially fire safety, which is a realistic concern for either a university or a boarding house.
